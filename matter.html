📘 Chapter 1: Matter in Our Surroundings

Definition of Matter

Everything in this universe that occupies space and has mass is called Matter.

✨ Examples of Matter

  • 🌬️ Air
  • 📚 Book
  • 🪑 Table
  • 🖊️ Pen

🔬 Physical Nature of Matter

Activity

  1. Take a 100 mL beaker.
  2. Fill it half with water.
  3. Mark the water level.
  4. Add some salt.
  5. Stir using a glass rod.
  6. Observe carefully.
Observation

Salt disappears after stirring because its particles mix with the particles of water.
Conclusion

Matter is made up of tiny particles.

🌿 Characteristics of Particles of Matter

  • Particles of matter have space between them.
  • Particles of matter are continuously moving.
  • Particles of matter attract each other.
  • The force of attraction varies from one substance to another.

Examples

  • Incense stick 🌸
  • Lemon juice 🍋
  • Coffee ☕
  • Tea 🍵

🌎 States of Matter

Matter mainly exists in five states.

    🪨 Solids

    💧 Liquids

    🌬️ Gases

  1. ⚡ Plasma
  2. ❄️ Bose–Einstein Condensate (BEC)

🪨 Solids

Definition

A solid has a definite shape, fixed volume and definite boundaries.

Examples

  • 📚 Book
  • 🪭 Fan
  • 🪨 Stone

Characteristics

  • Particles are closely packed.
  • Strong force of attraction.
  • Regular arrangement of particles.
  • High density.
  • Negligible compressibility.
  • Do not flow.
  • Very slow diffusion.

💧 Liquids

Definition

Liquids have a fixed volume but no fixed shape.

Examples

  • 💧 Water
  • 🛢️ Oil
  • 🥛 Milk

Characteristics

  • Can flow.
  • Called fluids.
  • No fixed shape.
  • Particles are less closely packed.
  • Moderate force of attraction.
  • Take the shape of the container.

🌬️ Gases

Definition

Gases have neither fixed shape nor fixed volume.

Characteristics

  • Very weak force of attraction.
  • Particles are far apart.
  • Lowest density.
  • Highly compressible.
  • Diffuse very quickly.

⚡ Plasma

Definition

Plasma is the fourth state of matter. It is an ionized gas whose particles are electrically charged.

Characteristics

  • ⚡ Particles are electrically charged.
  • 🔌 Good conductor of electricity.
  • 🧲 Affected by magnetic fields.
  • 🌬️ Has no fixed shape or volume.

Examples

  • ☀️ Sun
  • ⭐ Stars
  • ⚡ Lightning
  • 💡 Neon signs

❄️ Bose–Einstein Condensate (BEC)

Definition

BEC is the fifth state of matter formed at extremely low temperatures. In this state, particles come extremely close together and behave like a single unit.

Note: For Class 9, this is mainly for general knowledge.

💧 Water Exists in Three States

  • 🧊 Solid → Ice
  • 💧 Liquid → Water
  • 🌫️ Gas → Water Vapour

🌡️ Effect of Increase in Temperature

When a solid is heated:

  1. Particles gain kinetic energy.
  2. They vibrate faster.
  3. The force of attraction becomes weaker.
  4. Particles leave their fixed positions.
  5. The solid melts and changes into a liquid.

🧊 Melting Point

The melting point is the temperature at which a solid changes into a liquid at normal atmospheric pressure.

Example: Ice melts at 0°C (273.15 K).

🔥 Fusion (Melting)

The process of changing a solid into a liquid is called Fusion or Melting.

🔥 Latent Heat

Latent Heat is the heat absorbed or released during a change of state without any change in temperature.

Latent Heat of Fusion

The amount of heat required to convert 1 kg of a solid into liquid at its melting point without changing its temperature.

♨️ Effect of Heating a Liquid

  1. Particles gain more kinetic energy.
  2. They move faster.
  3. The force of attraction decreases.
  4. Particles escape into the air as gas.

💨 Boiling Point

The boiling point is the temperature at which a liquid changes into gas at normal atmospheric pressure.

Example: Water boils at 100°C (373 K).

Boiling is a bulk phenomenon.

🧊 Dry Ice

Dry Ice is the solid form of Carbon Dioxide (CO₂).

It changes directly from solid to gas without becoming liquid. This process is called Sublimation.

🌫️ Evaporation

Definition

Evaporation is the process by which a liquid changes into vapour at any temperature below its boiling point.

Evaporation takes place only at the surface of a liquid.

🧠 Why Does Evaporation Occur?

At any temperature, particles of a liquid have different amounts of kinetic energy. Some particles at the surface gain enough energy to overcome the force of attraction and escape into the air as vapour.

🌿 Factors Affecting Evaporation

1️⃣ Increase in Surface Area

A larger surface area exposes more particles to air, increasing the rate of evaporation.

Example: Wet clothes dry faster when spread out.

2️⃣ Increase in Temperature

When temperature increases, particles gain more kinetic energy and evaporate faster.

3️⃣ Decrease in Humidity

Humidity is the amount of water vapour present in the air.

When the air already contains a large amount of water vapour (high humidity), the rate of evaporation decreases.

4️⃣ Increase in Wind Speed

Wind carries away water vapour from the surface of the liquid, allowing more particles to escape. Therefore, evaporation increases.

Example: Clothes dry faster on a windy day.
